# Writing Style Reference

Use this when drafting or tightening memo language.

## Voice

Default voice: clear sponsor, not cheerleader; investor, not researcher.

The memo should sound like:

- "Here is why we like it."
- "Here is why the price can still work."
- "Here is what would make us wrong."
- "Here is why this is specifically attractive for our platform."

It should not sound like:

- a book report,
- a generic AI market overview,
- a consulting deck,
- or an over-hedged analyst note.

## Sentence Standards

Prefer short, direct sentences. Each paragraph should usually do one job.

Use:

- "Company materials show..."
- "Customer materials list..."
- "The watch-items are..."
- "The case for investing is..."
- "This is no longer early-entry pricing..."
- "The round gives investors follow-on exposure to..."

Avoid:

- "appears to sit at a real bottleneck"
- "blows them away"
- "must invest"
- "no-brainer"
- "category-defining" without proof
- "may be the cleanest..." when the memo has earned a firmer view
- "we discovered" unless the evidence supports it

## Bullish But Balanced

LP-facing memo language should be positive, clean, and grounded:

- "The reported growth profile is exceptional" is acceptable when numbers support it.
- "This is not early-entry pricing" is useful when paying up.
- "Watch-items" is better than "bear case" for sponsor memos.
- Put caveats next to the exact claim they qualify.

Bad:

> The company is exploding and could dominate AI.

Better:

> Company materials show growth from [A] to [B], alongside reported profitability. The question is whether that run-rate remains durable as customer concentration, gross margin, and product expansion are tested.

## Investment Thesis Examples

**Demand from the right buyers:** Customer materials list [buyer categories / approved names]. These are the budgets that matter most in [market].

**Exceptional growth with profitability:** Company materials show growth from [baseline] to [current run-rate], alongside reported profitability. That combination is rare for a company growing this quickly.

**Coherent product expansion:** [Product A] is the current core. [Product B] and [Product C] extend the same operating system into adjacent budgets rather than representing unrelated experiments.

**Differentiated access:** [Firm] first invested at [entry terms] and now has [allocation] into [round context]. This gives investors follow-on exposure to a company that has materially outgrown the original investment case.

**Exit potential:** As a buy-vs-build target, the company could be attractive to strategic buyers that may prefer to acquire scaled infrastructure rather than recreate it internally. If the company preserves neutrality across major customers, an IPO may be the cleaner long-term path.

## Risk Framing

Make risks specific:

- Customer concentration: what happens if the largest buyer pauses?
- Revenue quality: contracted recurring revenue vs annualized project run-rate.
- Gross margin: labor/services pass-through vs infrastructure leverage.
- In-housing: buyers may build internal teams.
- Synthetic substitution: AI-generated data may replace some human data.
- Security/trust: data provenance, IP leakage, compliance, government exposure.
- Product expansion: current core vs forecasted future products.

Avoid generic "competition is intense" unless paired with the real competitive axis.
