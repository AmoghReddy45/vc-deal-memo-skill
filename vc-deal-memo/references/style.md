# Writing Style Reference

Use this when drafting or tightening memo language.

## Voice

Default voice: clear sponsor, not cheerleader; investor, not researcher.

The memo should sound like:

- "Here is why we like it."
- "Here is why the price can still work."
- "Here is what would make us wrong."
- "Here is why this is specifically attractive for our platform."

It should not sound like:

- a book report,
- a generic AI market overview,
- a consulting deck,
- or an over-hedged analyst note.

## What Makes the Writing Work

The standard is not "professional enough." The standard is: a partner or LP can skim the page and immediately understand the investment judgment.

Good memo writing has:

- a clear point of view,
- plain English,
- specific nouns,
- short paragraphs,
- visible finance logic,
- and only the caveats that matter.

It should feel written by a human investor who has made a decision, not by a model summarizing inputs.

## Sentence Standards

Prefer short, direct sentences. Each paragraph should usually do one job.

Use:

- "Company materials show..."
- "Customer materials list..."
- "The watch-items are..."
- "The case for investing is..."
- "This is no longer early-entry pricing..."
- "The round gives investors follow-on exposure to..."
- "The case for participating is..."
- "The price can work if..."
- "The company has materially outgrown the original underwriting case."

Avoid:

- "appears to sit at a real bottleneck"
- "real bottleneck in frontier AI"
- "unlocking the future of..."
- "at the intersection of..."
- "poised to revolutionize..."
- "robust ecosystem"
- "mission-critical" unless it is specifically proven
- "blows them away"
- "must invest"
- "no-brainer"
- "category-defining" without proof
- "may be the cleanest..." when the memo has earned a firmer view
- "we discovered" unless the evidence supports it
- stacking "appears," "may," "could," and "suggests" in the same sentence
- em dashes. Use commas, periods, colons, semicolons, or parentheses instead. Em dashes often make the prose sound generic and over-styled.

## Rhythm and Paragraph Shape

Most paragraphs should be 1-3 sentences. A paragraph should not make the reader hold four ideas in memory.

Good pattern:

1. Start with the judgment.
2. Support it with one fact or mechanism.
3. Add the caveat only if it changes the investment decision.

Example:

> The reported growth profile is exceptional. Company materials show growth from [A] to [B], alongside reported profitability. The question is whether that run-rate remains durable as customer concentration and gross margin are tested.

Bad pattern:

> The company appears to be operating in a large and fast-growing market with strong tailwinds, and while there are risks around customer concentration and margin durability, it may be positioned to capture a meaningful share of the opportunity if it continues executing.

Why bad: it sounds generic, hides the judgment, and uses too many softeners.

## Rewrite Moves

When a sentence sounds AI-written, use these moves:

- Replace abstract nouns with the actual business mechanism.
- Replace "appears to be positioned to" with "is positioned to" only when supported, or "the case is" when it is interpretation.
- Move caveats to the end of the paragraph or the risk section.
- Use one strong verb instead of a phrase: "validates" over "serves as validation of."
- Remove throat-clearing: "It is important to note that," "In today's landscape," "As AI continues to evolve."
- Replace hype with price logic, buyer logic, or operating logic.

Before:

> The company appears to sit at a real bottleneck in frontier AI.

After:

> As models improve, the constraint shifts from generic data toward verified human expertise for evaluation, correction, and workflow feedback.

Before:

> The company is well-positioned to benefit from AI tailwinds.

After:

> The company sells into the budgets that matter most: [buyer categories], where model performance depends on expert evaluation and feedback.

Before:

> This valuation is attractive given the growth.

After:

> At [valuation], this is not early-entry pricing. The price can work if current run-rate quality, customer breadth, and margin durability hold.

## Bullish But Balanced

LP-facing memo language should be positive, clean, and grounded:

- "The reported growth profile is exceptional" is acceptable when numbers support it.
- "This is not early-entry pricing" is useful when paying up.
- "Watch-items" is better than "bear case" for sponsor memos.
- Put caveats next to the exact claim they qualify.

Bad:

> The company is exploding and could dominate AI.

Better:

> Company materials show growth from [A] to [B], alongside reported profitability. The question is whether that run-rate remains durable as customer concentration, gross margin, and product expansion are tested.

## Investment Thesis Examples

Use bold lead-in, colon, explanation. Keep the label tight and the explanation concrete.

**Demand from the right buyers:** Customer materials list [buyer categories / approved names]. These are the budgets that matter most in [market].

**Exceptional growth with profitability:** Company materials show growth from [baseline] to [current run-rate], alongside reported profitability. That combination is rare for a company growing this quickly.

**Coherent product expansion:** [Product A] is the current core. [Product B] and [Product C] extend the same operating system into adjacent budgets rather than representing unrelated experiments.

**Differentiated access:** [Firm] first invested at [entry terms] and now has [allocation] into [round context]. This gives investors follow-on exposure to a company that has materially outgrown the original investment case.

**Exit potential:** As a buy-vs-build target, the company could be attractive to strategic buyers that may prefer to acquire scaled infrastructure rather than recreate it internally. If the company preserves neutrality across major customers, an IPO may be the cleaner long-term path.

## Sponsor Voice vs Analyst Voice

Analyst voice:

> The company has shown strong traction, though risks remain.

Sponsor voice:

> The traction is strong enough to justify follow-on exposure. The key watch-items are customer concentration, run-rate quality, and margin durability.

Analyst voice:

> The product expansion could increase TAM.

Sponsor voice:

> The product expansion matters because it reuses the same operating system across adjacent buyer budgets.

Analyst voice:

> The valuation is high but potentially justified.

Sponsor voice:

> The round is not cheap. The case for participating is that scale, customer quality, and profitability may already support the cap.

## Risk Framing

Make risks specific:

- Customer concentration: what happens if the largest buyer pauses?
- Revenue quality: contracted recurring revenue vs annualized project run-rate.
- Gross margin: labor/services pass-through vs infrastructure leverage.
- In-housing: buyers may build internal teams.
- Synthetic substitution: AI-generated data may replace some human data.
- Security/trust: data provenance, IP leakage, compliance, government exposure.
- Product expansion: current core vs forecasted future products.

Avoid generic "competition is intense" unless paired with the real competitive axis.
