# Formatting and Presentation Reference

Use this when producing DOCX, PDF, or Google Docs-targeted memos.

## General Formatting

Default:

- Font: Arial.
- Body: 10-10.5 pt.
- Section headers: black or very dark navy bars with white bold text for the classic syndicate memo look. Use light gray table-header rows inside tables.
- Tables: thin borders, usually 0.5 pt. Avoid heavy gridlines.
- Header rows: consistent across all tables.
- Body copy: compact paragraphs, not dense walls of text.
- Sources: separate page or compact appendix; do not crowd the final recommendation.

## Visual Style

Default look: institutional, clean, slightly plain. The memo should feel like a high-trust investment document, not a pitch deck.

Use:

- black or near-black section bars,
- restrained gray table fills,
- thin lines,
- compact spacing,
- understated charts,
- strong labels,
- and limited color.

Avoid:

- bright accent colors,
- thick table borders,
- large decorative titles,
- gradient backgrounds,
- card-heavy layouts,
- oversized logos,
- pitchbook gloss,
- and inconsistent table styles.

The best visual test: the page should be easy to skim in 10 seconds, but still feel serious when printed.

## Tables

Use content-sized columns when columns have unequal density.

Good uses:

- deal terms,
- investment history,
- comps,
- financial snapshot,
- risk/watch-items,
- product mix,
- round details.

Avoid:

- equal-width columns when one column is a short label and another is long reasoning,
- right-aligned text headers unless all numeric,
- oversized rank columns,
- thick borders,
- inconsistent header fills.

For a top "Deal" table, a clean convention:

- "The Deal:" as text above the table.
- Left column `#d9d9d9`.
- Right column `#efefef`.
- No inner borders if visually clean.
- Thin 0.5 pt perimeter border.
- Labels in the left column should be bold.
- Keep row height tight but not cramped.

For standard tables:

- Header fill: light gray, usually `#d9d9d9` or `#e5e5e5`.
- Header text: black, bold, left aligned unless all entries are numeric.
- Body text: left aligned for prose, right aligned for numbers.
- Borders: 0.5 pt or lighter.
- Column widths: content-sized, not equal-width by default.

For comps tables:

- Include read-through, not just raw facts.
- Avoid fake precision.
- Use one row per comp, with the final column explaining what the comp means for the target.

For risk/watch-item tables:

- Keep the left label short.
- Put the actual analysis in the right column.
- Use "watch-item" language for LP memos and "diligence gate" language for internal memos.

## Charts

Charts should earn space in 3 seconds.

Useful chart types:

- reported run-rate growth,
- revenue by year,
- product-line mix,
- use of funds,
- comp valuation multiple.

Chart style:

- simple bar charts or line charts,
- dark navy or muted blue bars,
- light gray gridlines,
- direct data labels for the important points,
- no 3D effects,
- no decorative backgrounds,
- no excessive legends,
- and a small source/basis note below.

Prefer a chart when it communicates the point faster than a paragraph. Use a table when exact values matter more than shape.

Always add a basis footnote:

> Source: company-provided materials. Gross annual run-rate is annualized current-period activity, not GAAP revenue or contracted SaaS ARR.

## Section Flow and Page Layout

For sponsor memos, the page order should make the investor decision feel natural:

1. overview,
2. deal terms,
3. investment history or why now,
4. thesis,
5. comps/pricing,
6. traction,
7. market/product/team,
8. watch-items,
9. conclusion,
10. round details.

Keep important section headers with the content that follows. Do not let a section bar sit alone at the bottom of a page.

Put the most decision-relevant chart or table early, usually after Overview or Traction. Do not bury the central evidence in an appendix.

## Layout QA

Before finalizing:

- Render DOCX to page images or PDF.
- Check that section headers are not orphaned.
- Check charts/images render in the target format.
- Check page breaks around tables.
- Check title size and weight.
- Check header/footer/logo imports correctly if using Google Docs.
- If images break in Google Docs, use embedded body images and avoid fragile headers or linked images.
